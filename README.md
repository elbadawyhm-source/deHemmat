Chapter 1: Fundamentals of Volumetric Analysis

1.1 What is Volumetric Analysis?
Volumetric analysis (also called titrimetry) is a quantitative analytical technique that determines the concentration of an unknown solution by measuring the volume of a known solution required to complete a chemical reaction.

?? DEFINITION
Titration: The process of carefully adding a solution of known concentration (the titrant) from a burette to a solution of unknown concentration (the analyte) until the reaction is complete.
Standard Solution: A solution whose concentration is accurately known.
Equivalence Point: The point at which the titrant has reacted completely with the analyte in stoichiometric proportions.
End Point: The observable change (usually a colour change from an indicator) that signals the equivalence point has been reached.
1.2 Why Use Volumetric Analysis?
Volumetric analysis is widely used in science and industry because it offers:
• Accuracy & Precision: Well-performed titrations can achieve errors of less than 0.1%
• Speed: A single titration can be completed in minutes once solutions are prepared
• Simplicity: Requires relatively inexpensive glassware and reagents
• Versatility: Applicable to acid-base, redox, precipitation, and complexometric reactions

2.1 Acid-Base Reactions
A neutralization reaction occurs when an acid reacts with a base to form a salt and water:

Acid + Base  ?  Salt + Water
At the ionic level, all neutralization reactions reduce to:

2.2 Stoichiometry of Neutralization
Stoichiometry tells us the ratio in which reactants combine. For titration:

?? DEFINITION
Mole Ratio: The ratio of moles of acid to moles of base, derived from the balanced chemical equation.
The general titration relationship is:
nA / nB = a / b
Where nA = moles of acid, nB = moles of base, a = stoichiometric coefficient of acid, b = stoichiometric coefficient of base.

?? WORKED EXAMPLE
Example: In the reaction H?SO? + 2NaOH ? Na?SO? + 2H?O, the mole ratio is 1:2 (acid:base).
If 0.01 mol of H?SO? is used, then 0.02 mol of NaOH is required for complete neutralization.

2.4 The pH Curve (Titration Curve)
As titrant is added, the pH of the solution changes. Plotting pH against volume of titrant added produces a characteristic S-shaped curve called the titration curve.

Key Features of a Strong Acid–Strong Base Curve (e.g., HCl vs. NaOH):
• Initial pH is low (the acid solution, ~pH 1–2)
• pH rises slowly at first as base is added
• A very steep rise occurs near the equivalence point
• Equivalence point for strong acid vs. strong base: pH = 7.0
• pH levels off at high values as excess base is added (~pH 12–13)

Chapter 3: Standard Solutions and Their Preparation

3.1 What is a Standard Solution?
?? DEFINITION
Primary Standard: A substance that can be used to prepare a standard solution directly by dissolving a weighed amount in a known volume of solvent. Must be: highly pure, stable, high molar mass, non-hygroscopic, and readily soluble.
Secondary Standard: A solution standardised by titrating it against a primary standard. Its concentration is not known precisely until it is calibrated.

3.3 Preparing a Standard Solution: Step-by-Step
Example: Preparing 250.0 mL of 0.100 mol/L sodium carbonate (Na?CO?) standard solution

1. Calculate mass required: n = c × V = 0.100 mol/L × 0.2500 L = 0.02500 mol; m = n × Mr = 0.02500 × 105.99 = 2.650 g
2. Dry the primary standard: Heat Na?CO? in an oven at 270°C for 1 hour to remove moisture; cool in a desiccator.
3. Weigh accurately: Weigh ? 2.650 g using a 4-decimal-place analytical balance. Record exact mass.
4. Dissolve: Transfer to a 250 mL beaker. Add ~100 mL distilled water. Stir to dissolve completely.
5. Transfer to volumetric flask: Carefully pour into a 250 mL volumetric flask. Rinse beaker 3× with small volumes of distilled water and add washings to flask.
6. Make up to the mark: Add distilled water slowly until the bottom of the meniscus sits exactly on the 250.0 mL graduation mark. Stopper and invert 10 times to mix.
7. Recalculate exact concentration: c = m / (Mr × V) using the actual mass weighed.

?? KEY POINT
Always recalculate the exact concentration using the mass actually weighed rather than the target mass. The concentration may differ slightly from the nominal value.
3.4 Standardisation of NaOH
NaOH cannot be a primary standard because it is hygroscopic and absorbs CO? from air. Instead, its concentration is determined by titration against a primary standard (e.g., KHP):

KHC?H?O? + NaOH ? KNaC?H?O? + H?O  (1:1 mole ratio)
Since the mole ratio is 1:1, the concentration of NaOH can be found directly:
n(KHP) = n(NaOH)  ?  c(NaOH) = n(KHP) / V(NaOH)



Chapter 4: Titration Procedures and Techniques

4.1 Setting Up the Burette
8. Clamp the burette vertically on a retort stand.
9. Rinse the burette with 3 portions (~5 mL each) of the titrant solution, allowing each to drain through the tap. Discard the rinsings. This removes any residual water that would dilute the titrant.
10. Fill the burette with the titrant above the 0.00 mL mark.
11. Open the tap briefly to fill the tip and expel any air bubbles.
12. Read the initial burette reading at the bottom of the meniscus to the nearest 0.05 mL. Record this value.

?? WATCH OUT
Never fill a burette from the top while holding it upright without a funnel, as spillage and uneven filling can introduce errors. Always check for air bubbles in the tip before starting.
4.2 Setting Up the Conical Flask
13. Rinse the conical flask with distilled water (not the analyte solution!).
14. Use a pipette to transfer exactly 25.00 mL of the analyte into the flask.
15. Add 2–3 drops of the appropriate indicator.
16. Place the flask on a white tile under the burette tip.

?? KEY POINT
The conical flask does NOT need to be rinsed with the analyte because you are using a pipette to measure a fixed number of moles (not a fixed concentration). Adding distilled water only changes volume, not the number of moles.
4.3 The Titration Procedure
Rough Titration (Trial Run): Perform a fast first titration to estimate the end point volume.
17. Open the tap and add titrant quickly, swirling constantly.
18. Watch for the indicator colour change. When the colour starts to persist, slow down.
19. Continue until a permanent colour change appears. Record the final burette reading.
20. Calculate a rough titre: Rough Titre = Final Reading ? Initial Reading

Accurate Titrations (at least 3): Repeat the procedure with precision.
21. Refill the burette and re-zero to 0.00 mL (or record exact starting value).
22. Add titrant quickly until ~1 mL before the rough titre volume.
23. Slow down to drop-by-drop addition. Swirl after each drop.
24. Stop at the first permanent colour change that lasts ? 30 seconds. Record reading to ±0.05 mL.
25. Repeat until you have three concordant results (within 0.10 mL of each other).
26. Calculate the mean titre from concordant results only.

4.4 Reading the Burette
Always read the burette at eye level. For colourless solutions, read the bottom of the meniscus. For coloured solutions (e.g., permanganate), read the top of the meniscus.

Chapter 5: Indicators in Neutralization Titration

5.1 What is an Indicator?
?? DEFINITION
An indicator is a weak acid or weak base that has a different colour in its protonated (HIn) and deprotonated (In?) forms. The colour change occurs over a specific pH range known as the transition range or colour change interval.
The indicator equilibrium can be written as:
HIn  ?  H? + In?
(Colour 1)        (Colour 2)
?? KEY POINT
An indicator should only be used in 2–3 drops. Too much indicator can introduce a significant error because it will consume some of the titrant to produce its own colour change.

Chapter 6: Titration Calculations

6.1 The Calculation Framework
Titration calculations follow a logical 5-step process:

Step
Action
Formula/Tool
1
Write and balance the chemical equation
Balanced equation gives mole ratios
2
Calculate moles of the known substance
n = c × V (use volume in litres!)
3
Use mole ratio to find moles of unknown
n(unknown) = n(known) × (ratio)
4
Calculate concentration or mass of unknown
c = n/V  OR  m = n × Mr
5
Check units and significant figures
Report to 3–4 significant figures typically
6.2 Worked Examples
Example 1: Simple 1:1 Ratio — Finding Concentration of NaOH
?? WORKED EXAMPLE
Problem: 25.00 mL of NaOH solution required 22.40 mL of 0.100 mol/L HCl to neutralize. Find the concentration of NaOH.

Step 1 — Balanced equation: HCl + NaOH ? NaCl + H?O  (mole ratio 1:1)
Step 2 — Moles of HCl: n(HCl) = c × V = 0.100 mol/L × 0.02240 L = 2.240 × 10?³ mol
Step 3 — Moles of NaOH: n(NaOH) = n(HCl) × (1/1) = 2.240 × 10?³ mol
Step 4 — Concentration of NaOH: c(NaOH) = n / V = 2.240 × 10?³ / 0.02500 = 0.0896 mol/L
Answer: c(NaOH) = 0.0896 mol/L
Example 2: 1:2 Ratio — Finding Concentration of H?SO?
?? WORKED EXAMPLE
Problem: 20.00 mL of H?SO? required 36.75 mL of 0.200 mol/L NaOH to neutralize. Find the concentration of H?SO?.

Step 1 — Balanced equation: H?SO? + 2NaOH ? Na?SO? + 2H?O  (mole ratio 1:2)
Step 2 — Moles of NaOH: n(NaOH) = 0.200 × 0.03675 = 7.350 × 10?³ mol
Step 3 — Moles of H?SO?: n(H?SO?) = n(NaOH) × (1/2) = 7.350 × 10?³ / 2 = 3.675 × 10?³ mol
Step 4 — Concentration: c(H?SO?) = 3.675 × 10?³ / 0.02000 = 0.184 mol/L
Answer: c(H?SO?) = 0.184 mol/L
Example 3: Finding Mass Percentage in a Sample
?? WORKED EXAMPLE
Problem: A 1.500 g sample of impure oxalic acid (H?C?O?, Mr = 90.03 g/mol) was dissolved in water and titrated against 0.100 mol/L NaOH. The titre was 28.60 mL. Calculate the percentage purity of the sample.

Step 1 — Balanced equation: H?C?O? + 2NaOH ? Na?C?O? + 2H?O  (ratio 1:2)
Step 2 — Moles of NaOH: n(NaOH) = 0.100 × 0.02860 = 2.860 × 10?³ mol
Step 3 — Moles of H?C?O?: n(H?C?O?) = 2.860 × 10?³ / 2 = 1.430 × 10?³ mol
Step 4 — Mass of pure H?C?O?: m = 1.430 × 10?³ × 90.03 = 0.1287 g
Step 5 — Percentage purity: % purity = (0.1287 / 1.500) × 100 = 8.58%
Answer: The sample is 8.58% pure oxalic acid. (The rest is impurity.)
6.3 Back Titration
?? DEFINITION
Back Titration: Used when a direct titration is impractical — for example, when the analyte is insoluble, reacts slowly, or has no suitable indicator. A known excess of reagent is added to the analyte, and the remaining (unreacted) excess is titrated with a second reagent.
Formula: n(analyte) = n(reagent added) ? n(reagent remaining)

?? WORKED EXAMPLE
Problem: 2.00 g of impure CaCO? (Mr = 100.09) was added to 50.00 mL of 0.500 mol/L HCl. The excess HCl was titrated with 0.250 mol/L NaOH; titre = 14.40 mL. Find the percentage of CaCO? in the sample.

Reactions: 
CaCO? + 2HCl ? CaCl? + H?O + CO?  (reaction with excess acid)
HCl + NaOH ? NaCl + H?O  (back titration of excess acid)

n(HCl) total added = 0.500 × 0.05000 = 0.02500 mol
n(NaOH) used in back titration = 0.250 × 0.01440 = 3.600 × 10?³ mol
n(HCl) in back titration = n(NaOH) = 3.600 × 10?³ mol (1:1 ratio)
n(HCl) reacted with CaCO? = 0.02500 ? 3.600 × 10?³ = 0.02140 mol
n(CaCO?) = 0.02140 / 2 = 0.01070 mol  (1:2 ratio from equation)
m(CaCO?) = 0.01070 × 100.09 = 1.071 g
% CaCO? = (1.071 / 2.00) × 100 = 53.6%

7.3 Evaluating Precision and Accuracy
After completing your titration:
• Precision: Assessed by the spread of concordant titres. Three titres within 0.10 mL of each other indicate good precision.
• Accuracy: Assessed by comparing your result to the known or accepted value. Calculate % error:
% Error = |(Experimental ? Accepted)| / Accepted × 100%
• Significant Figures: Report concentrations to 3 or 4 significant figures, matching the precision of your burette (±0.05 mL on a 50 mL burette = 4 significant figures in a 25 mL titre).



Chapter 8: Real-World Case Study — Pharmaceutical Quality Control

Determining the Acetylsalicylic Acid Content of Aspirin Tablets Using Back Titration

8.1 Context and Problem Statement
Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid, ASA) is one of the world's most widely consumed pharmaceuticals. A standard 500 mg aspirin tablet is required by pharmacopoeia (official pharmaceutical standards) to contain between 95% and 105% of its stated dose of ASA.

Quality control chemists at a pharmaceutical manufacturing facility receive a batch of newly manufactured aspirin tablets and must verify that the ASA content falls within the acceptable range before the batch can be released for sale. This is a real-world application of neutralization titration, specifically back titration, because:
• ASA reacts slowly with NaOH at room temperature (direct titration would be unreliable)
• The reaction between ASA and NaOH requires heating to go to completion quickly
• A back titration with a heated excess of NaOH provides accurate, reproducible results

8.2 Chemistry of the Reaction
Acetylsalicylic acid is a diprotic acid (has two acidic groups), but under the conditions used, the acetyl group is also hydrolysed by NaOH, making it effectively a triprotic system:

?? CASE STUDY
Step 1 — Hydrolysis and neutralization with excess NaOH:
C?H?O? + 3NaOH ? C?H?O?Na + CH?COONa + 2H?O
(Aspirin + sodium hydroxide ? sodium salicylate + sodium acetate + water)

Step 2 — Back titration of excess NaOH with HCl:
NaOH + HCl ? NaCl + H?O
8.3 Experimental Procedure
Materials
• 5 aspirin tablets (stated: 500 mg each)
• 0.5000 mol/L NaOH solution (standardized against KHP)
• 0.2500 mol/L HCl solution (standardized against Na?CO?)
• Phenolphthalein indicator
• 50 mL burette, 25 mL pipette, 250 mL volumetric flask, 250 mL conical flask
• Water bath or hot plate

Procedure
27. Weigh 5 tablets accurately. Total mass: 2.587 g. Mean mass per tablet: 0.5174 g.
28. Crush tablets in a mortar and pestle. Transfer all powder to a 250 mL conical flask.
29. Add exactly 50.00 mL of 0.5000 mol/L NaOH using a pipette to the flask. Record: n(NaOH) added = 0.5000 × 0.05000 = 0.02500 mol
30. Gently heat the mixture on a water bath at 70°C for 15 minutes with swirling to ensure complete hydrolysis. Cool to room temperature.
31. Add 3 drops of phenolphthalein indicator (solution should be pink at this stage — confirms NaOH is still in excess).
32. Back-titrate the excess NaOH with standardized 0.2500 mol/L HCl from a burette.
33. Perform three accurate titrations and record concordant results.

n(NaOH)reacted = 0.02500 ? 4.613 × 10?³ = 0.02039 mol

Step 4 — Moles of aspirin (3 mol NaOH reacts with 1 mol ASA):
n(ASA) = n(NaOH)reacted / 3 = 0.02039 / 3 = 6.796 × 10?³ mol

Step 5 — Mass of ASA in the 5-tablet sample (Mr of ASA = 180.16 g/mol):
m(ASA) = n × Mr = 6.796 × 10?³ × 180.16 = 1.224 g

Step 6 — Mass of ASA per tablet:
m per tablet = 1.224 g / 5 tablets = 0.2448 g = 244.8 mg

Step 7 — Percentage of stated dose:
% of stated dose = (244.8 mg / 500 mg) × 100 = 48.96%
8.6 Interpretation of Results
The experimental result of 244.8 mg per tablet (48.96% of stated dose) is significantly below the pharmacopoeial specification of 475–525 mg (95–105%).

10.4 Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
Common Mistake
Correct Approach
Forgetting to convert mL to L in n = c × V
Always divide mL by 1000: 25.00 mL = 0.02500 L
Using 1:1 ratio for diprotic acids (H?SO?)
Always write and balance the equation first; H?SO? + 2NaOH
Including rough titration in the mean
Only average concordant titres from accurate runs
Saying 'end point = equivalence point'
They are different: end point is observed, equivalence point is theoretical
Not reporting uncertainties or sig figs
Match sig figs to your least precise measurement (usually the burette: ±0.05 mL)
Confusing primary and secondary standards
Primary standards have accurately known purity; secondary standards must be calibrated

Term
Definition
